App Kickoff

Like with most large scope projects, proper management and planning plays a huge roll in the success of the final product. At UVU I spent a year working on a team project with clients that had the goal of starting a foundation that would help out local musicians develop their business and music skills to help take the next step in their music careers.


Starting from scratch meant not only designing and developing a website, but also planning out what features would help the foundation get off the ground as well.

Getting Started

Identifying the Problem

Utah lacks a place for locals musicians and artists to develop their skills to progress their careers both in talent and understanding of the music industry. A foundation can provide the means for people to achieve their goals and meet others in the music industry.

Foundation goals

Starting off there were a handful of goals the clients already had in mind and wanted to achieve which included:


  • Raise money to build a facility
  • Provide online courses as well as in person courses at the facility once built
  • Have a website that can easily be updated to reflect what the foundation is doing
  • Provide a way for musicians to be able to sell merchandise online

Strategy

Create a website to help the foundation meet their goals. The site will help hit these goals by having:


  • A Donation System to raise money
  • Online courses
  • blog/news page to keep donors up to date
  • Online store which can easily be managed by those running the foundation

Research

Luckily our clients had a lot of info for us to work with when it came to planning out who their target audience was. We decided it would be a good idea to limit our targeted audience or personas down to three people to keep things focused. Each persona represented someone that the foundation we be working with wether it was musicians, students or potential donors.

Planning

Structure and Hierarchy

Now that we had a rough idea of the goals of the website, along with who the audience was, the next step was to start building site maps and wireframes to plan out the structure of the site. We wanted to get a sense of what specific pages would be needed for the site, as well as what info would make the most sense on each page.

Since the foundation was just getting started, the goal was to make donations a key aspect of the site. As far as the hierarch totem pole goes, donations was meant be right at the top along with displaying who the foundations was and what they’re planning.

Website Phases

It became pretty clear early on that there would need to be different phases of the website being that the goals needed to be accomplished loosely in order. An example of this is the online store which sells local artists merchandise. Without having the foundation up and running, or having products to list on the store, it didn’t make a whole lot of sense to create a store right off the bat.


We ended up planning on three phases for the site which each focused on different goals of the foundation.

Communication & Importance of a plan

So far everything had been moving quite smooth with the project, we had a good idea of what they were trying to accomplish, who our target audience was, and how the website should be implemented. Now was the time to get in and start building the website based on the plan which had been laid out.

Anticipating Changes

Being this was the first time working with an actual client for many of the members on the team, something a lot of us didn’t foresee was the clients desire to make changes or updates to the site after the development phase had started. At first it didn’t seem like anything to worry about, but quickly got out of hand. Pushing back on the clients to not make so many changes seemed difficult at the time but may have benefited the project looking back on it… more on this later.

"Plans are things that change"

-Fujio Cho

Objectives and Benchmarks

One way we could have helped keep the project more on course would have been to set clearer expectations with our client, and ourselves earlier on in the project. A good way of accomplishing this would have been setting tasks and objectives at the begging of our weekly meetings to keep everything on topic, as well as more clearly defined bench marks for the site to mark its progress.

Adapting to changes

Focusing on the project goal

Originally the plan we set out on was to focus on phase one of the website, making sure that was fully realized and thought out before moving onto phase two or three. After some derailment, the new plan was to build all three phases of the site so everything was ready to go from day one, and phases could be added to the site at the clients discretion.


In theory, a setup where the client has everything at their fingertips right off the bat is great, but may not be super practical in the long term. In reality it may be a year, if not several before anything like a store needs to be implemented or in used on the site at which time may have different requirements than what we built.

Why this is important

With how competitive the digital media space is, we needed to not only create a site that met our users needs, but was something that kept users engaged and interested. Had the scope of the project been kept in individual phases, more time could have been spent focusing on delivering a product that excelled at what we were trying to offer users, rather than worrying about getting everything in before the deadline

“You can’t be everything to everyone but you can be something to someone”

-Andrew Davis

Getting caught in the weeds early on, worrying about adding extra features in some ways watered down the potency of the site. Yes it has the features the client was looking for, but doesn’t deliver them in a meaningful rememberable way to the users.

Key takeaways

I learned a lot over the course of this project, primarily about the dynamic of working with a client and a team. Learning when and when not to push back as a developer & designer is important and at times uncomfortable, but ultimately can be beneficial for you, the project and the client being that it could mean the final product is more in line with what’s achievable and meets the clients needs.
